# 【Abstract】

【Abstract】Freezing frogs is the most widespread and yet most controversial everyday activity among the ice fairies around Lake Misty. Historically, this practice has relied on the individual experience of the caster, with no systematic parametric analysis or theoretical characterization, which leads to an unstable trade-off between freezing success rate and frog survival. To address this issue, this thesis proposes a Controlled Cryogenic Freezing of Amphibians (CCFA) framework whose core control variables are cold-air intensity $\theta$, exposure duration $\tau$, and target posture angle $\alpha$. The framework first formalizes the ice fairy's cold emission as a directional conical cold source, and derives closed-form conditions for frog epidermal phase transition under environmental parameters typical of Lake Misty. Building on this foundation, an attitude-conditioned progressive attenuation strategy is designed so that ice-crystal growth stays synchronized with the biological stress response, achieving global freezing without endangering individual survival. Theoretical analysis, grounded in the principle of least action, provides upper and lower bounds on the success rate, and proves that under a fixed product constraint on $\theta$ and $\tau$, frogs with posture angle $\alpha<\pi/6$ attain near-optimal freezing efficiency. Experiments conducted at three representative environments—the pond in front of Hakurei Shrine, the stream of Youkai Mountain, and the yard of the House of the Lost—cover six species and a total of 810 trials. The results show that, compared with the traditional free-release baseline, CCFA improves the overall success rate by 23.7% and the frog revival rate by 11.4%, with a significant reduction in variance. This work provides the first systematic engineering description of ice-fairy freezing practice, and offers a reusable methodological path for research on the coupling between phase transition and magical energy in Gensokyo biophysics.

【Key words】ice fairy; frog freezing; cold-air modulation; phase-transition thermodynamics; behavioral control
